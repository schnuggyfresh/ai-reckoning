First, understand the two strongest cases.

The Case for Abundance

Every automation wave in history has created more jobs than it destroyed. When coal engines became efficient, total coal use went up (Jevons' paradox). When ATMs replaced tellers, banks opened more branches and hired more people. AI makes intelligence cheap — which expands what's economically possible. New industries, new services, new forms of demand we can't yet imagine. Andreessen: "Human wants and needs are endless, and entrepreneurs continuously create new goods and services." Citadel's rebuttal to Citrini notes software engineer demand is up 11% YoY and new business formation is expanding. Bloch's "2028 Intelligence Boom" models the same AI assumptions as Citrini but shows household purchasing power improving as costs collapse faster than incomes fall.

Andreessen, Citadel/Flight, Bloch, Jevons, Lieberman

The Case for Crisis

Previous waves automated muscles or narrow tasks. AI automates cognition itself — the one thing workers were supposed to retrain into. METR data shows AI task completion doubling every 7 months. Block just cut 4,000 workers from a position of strength, and the stock jumped 24%. Citrini's "Intelligence Displacement Spiral" describes a feedback loop: layoffs → less spending → more AI investment → more layoffs. "Ghost GDP" — output that shows up in national accounts but never circulates because machines spend zero on discretionary goods. Amodei warns 50% of entry-level white-collar jobs could go within 5 years. Shumer: "I describe what I want built, and it just appears. The finished thing."
